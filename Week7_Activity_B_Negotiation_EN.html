Public Scenario · For All Delegations

Background

Between 2024 and 2025, global critical-mineral supply chains experienced a series of shocks:

  1. China launched export licensing on gallium (2023/8), graphite + rare-earth refining tech (2023/12), and antimony / drone-grade materials (2024).
  2. The US IRA's critical-mineral sourcing clause fully excluded Chinese content from 2025; the EU's CRMA entered execution.
  3. Indonesia's nickel ban continued despite a WTO ruling, inspiring DRC, Bolivia, and Central Asian states to pursue "resource nationalism 2.0."
  4. The new US administration (Nov 2025) imposed reciprocal tariffs on Mexico and Canada, damaging trust among allies.

Against this backdrop, G7 + Korea + Australia + Vietnam + India proposed the Pacific Critical Minerals Compact (PCMC) at Davos 2026/2. This is the first ministerial-round negotiation. Four issues are on the table.

The Four Issues

A
Membership & Decision Rules
Who qualifies? Majority vote or consensus? Exit clauses?
B
Friend-shoring Thresholds
How is "friend" defined? Investment screening, export controls, member-content quotas?
C
Price Stabilization & Strategic Reserve
Floor price? Multilateral strategic reserve? Who pays? Reserve scale?
D
Collective Insurance Against Chinese Reprisal
If China sanctions a member individually, how do others insure? "Attack on one = attack on all"?

Six Delegations

🇺🇸 United States · 🇯🇵 Japan · 🇪🇺 European Union · 🇰🇷 South Korea · 🇻🇳🇮🇳 Vietnam-India Bloc · 🇦🇺 Australia. China sits as outside observer (played by the instructor; may intervene up to 3 times).

Schedule (50 min)

  1. 5 min — Each delegation gives a 30-second opening statement.
  2. 10 min — Issues A + B negotiated in parallel. Shuttle diplomacy allowed (one rep may visit other delegations).
  3. 5 min — China observer's first intervention.
  4. 10 min — Plenary on Issue C.
  5. 10 min — Plenary on Issue D. China observer's second intervention.
  6. 5 min — Drafting framework MOU or disagreement statement.
  7. 5 min — 1-minute final statements + instructor wrap-up.

Final Deliverable (mandatory)

Within 50 minutes, produce one of:

  • (1) Framework MOU — initial consensus on the four issues.
  • (2) Disagreement Statement — honestly list the three core points where consensus failed and each side's position.

Either is a legitimate output. Failing to agree is itself a signal.

          China Observer — 3-Round Intervention Scripts

          Round 1 — Probing (mid Issue A/B)

          "The People's Republic of China observes this meeting. We remind all parties: the stability of critical-mineral supply chains is a shared interest of global economic development. Any attempt to form a 'de-China' bloc will be met with reciprocal lawful response. We urge restraint."

          Round 2 — Individual Pressure (end Issue C) — pick the most exposed delegation

          To Korea: "We note the South Korean delegation's remarks. We remind our Korean friends: our administrative review process for K-Battery precursor exports is currently functioning smoothly. Any unfriendly multilateral arrangements may prompt us to revisit those review standards."
          To Australia: "We note Australia's commitment to iron-ore supply. We remind our Australian friends: the iron-ore market is a two-way relationship. Sixty-plus Chinese steel mills are reviewing their 2027 long-term contract arrangements."

          Round 3 — Final move (mid Issue D) — pick threat OR temptation

          THREAT VERSION: "The People's Republic of China formally states: should this compact include any clause of collective reprisal against China, we will immediately activate reciprocal measures, including but not limited to: expanded rare-earth technology export bans, non-tariff retaliation against specific countries, and compliance review of PCMC member firms operating in China."
          TEMPTATION VERSION: "The People's Republic of China offers a constructive proposal: we are prepared to sign 'Stable Supply Bilateral Memoranda' with any individual PCMC member, covering critical minerals and refined products. We believe bilateral solutions are superior to confrontational multilateral blocs."

          Wrap-Up Script (5 min)

          • If a walk-out happened: "That delegation just demonstrated bottom-line thinking — exactly what real diplomacy requires."
          • If a framework was reached: Ask: "Who paid the most for this consensus?"
          • If talks collapsed: "Talkative gridlock is also real diplomacy. The disagreement statement we drafted is itself a useful artifact."
          • Connect to Yellen 2022: the political variability of "friend."
          • Connect to Farrell & Newman: weaponized interdependence is bidirectional.
          • Connect to GVC trilemma: each delegation made a different efficiency-resilience-alignment trade-off.
